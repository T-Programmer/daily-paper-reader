Abstract 

Recent advances in vision-language models (VLMs) have enabled broad progress in the general medical field. How-ever, pathology still remains a more challenging sub-domain, with current pathology-specific VLMs exhibiting limitations in both diagnostic accuracy and reasoning plausibility. Such shortcomings are largely attributable to the nature of cur-rent pathology datasets, which are primarily composed of image–description pairs that lack the depth and structured diagnostic paradigms employed by real-world pathologists. In this study, we leverage pathology textbooks and real-world pathology experts to construct high-quality, reasoning-oriented datasets. Building on this, we introduce Patho-R1, a multimodal RL-based pathology Reasoner, trained through a three-stage pipeline: (1) continued pretraining on 3.5 mil-lion image-text pairs for knowledge infusion; (2) supervised fine-tuning on 500k high-quality Chain-of-Thought samples for reasoning incentivizing; (3) reinforcement learning using Group Relative Policy Optimization and Decoupled Clip and Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization strategies for mul-timodal reasoning quality refinement. To further assess the alignment quality of our dataset, we propose Patho-CLIP, trained on the same figure-caption corpus used for contin-ued pretraining. Comprehensive experimental results demon-strate that both Patho-CLIP and Patho-R1 achieve robust per-formance across a wide range of pathology-related tasks, in-cluding zero-shot classification, cross-modal retrieval, Visual Question Answering, and Multiple Choice Question.

However, build-ing robust AI systems for pathology presents significantly greater challenges compared to other medical imaging tasks like MRI (Wu et al. 2023) or CT (Wu, Zhuang, and Chen 2024). This stems from the inherent complexity and fine-grained nature of pathology data, coupled with the scarcity of high-quality, expert-annotated multimodal datasets (Xie et al. 2024). While general vision-language models (VLMs), such as CLIP (Radford et al. 2021) and LLaVA (Liu et al. 2023), have shown promising results across various med-ical imaging tasks (Wang et al. 2022; Moor et al. 2023), their performance in pathology remains limited, especially in terms of cross-disease generalization and the comprehen-sion of complex diagnostic tasks, highlighting the urgent need for pathology-specific solutions (Moor et al. 2023; Li et al. 2023). Existing datasets, sourced from educa-tional videos, social media, and institutional archives, tend to favor surface-level descriptions over semantically rich, diagnostically grounded interpretations. “Textbook-quality” data (Gunasekar et al. 2023), encompassing detailed disease mechanisms and organized domain knowledge, could enable models to reason more clinically. Notably, recent success in reinforcement learning (RL), exemplified by DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al. 2025) and OpenAI o1 (Jaech et al. 2024), highlights RL’s potential in enhancing reasoning within language models. Among existing meth-ods, the Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) stands out for its simple but effective ground truth-driven reward design, and has been validated in logic-intensive tasks like math and coding, as well as in domains requiring systematic thinking (Huang et al. 2025; Yang et al. 2025). ## Related Work 

Large Vision Language Models in Medical Fields. The advent of general-purpose VLMs such as CLIP (Radford et al. 2021) has propelled multimodal progress in the med-ical domain. To better capture domain-specific semantics, various CLIP-based adaptations have been proposed, includ-ing PubMedCLIP (Eslami, Meinel, and De Melo 2023), MedCLIP (Wang et al. 2022), UniMed-CLIP (Khattak et al. 2024), and Med-PaLM (Singhal et al. 2023); for computa-tional pathology in particular, specialized variants such as PLIP (Huang et al. 2023), CONCH (Lu et al. 2024a), MUSK (Xiang et al. 2025), TITAN (Ding et al. 2024), and Quilt-Net (Ikezogwo et al. 2023) further tailor the image–text pre-training paradigm to histopathology images and reports. To further enhance the utility of medical VLMs, a new line of multimodal medical assistants has emerged, including Med-Flamingo (Moor et al. 2023), LLaVA-Med (Li et al. 2023), RadFM (Wu et al. 2023), and HuatuoGPT-Vision (Chen et al. 2024); their pathology-centric derivatives, including PathAsst (Sun et al. 2024b), Quilt-LLaVA (Seyfioglu et al. 2024), PathChat (Lu et al. 2024b), PathGen-LLaVA (Sun et al. 2025), and PA-LLaVA (Dai et al. 2024). These models use large language models for image-conditioned dialogue and question answering, enhancing interactive and context-aware medical understanding. 

Vision-Language Reinforcement Learning for Post-Training. Recently, the focus of large language model (LLM) research has been shifting from traditional super-vised learning to RL as a means to enhance reasoning ca-pabilities (Guo et al. 2025; Jaech et al. 2024). One line of work focuses on Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, which involves creating structured multimodal reasoning datasets and using instruction tuning to enhance reasoning traceabil-ity, as seen in LLaVA-CoT (Xu et al. 2024) and LlamaV-o1 (Thawakar et al. 2025). In contrast, approaches like Visual-RFT (Liu et al. 2025), VLM-R1 (Shen et al. 2025), and Perception-R1 (Yu et al. 2025a) bypass CoT-style tuning and instead apply reinforcement learning to directly im-prove visual perception, guided by carefully designed re-ward functions under unsupervised or weakly supervised settings. Building on this, methods such as R1-OneVision (Yang et al. 2025), R1-V (Chen et al. 2025), and Vision-R1 (Huang et al. 2025) introduce a novel cross-modal reason-ing pipeline: they first convert images into formalized lan-guage representations capturing visual semantics, then con-struct language-centric visual reasoning datasets for instruc-tion tuning, followed by an RL phase to incentivize deeper multimodal reasoning. While these methods have achieved notable progress in general domains, their application to medical imaging remains nascent. ## Method 

Dataset Overview 

As VLMs continue to show promise in domain-specific ap-plications, the construction of high-quality datasets becomes increasingly vital for developing clinically meaningful mod-els. However, existing datasets face two key limitations: (1) they primarily focus on general medical domains, lack-ing coverage of pathology-specific data; and (2) they often omit detailed, authoritative diagnostic reasoning, empha-sizing only brief descriptions and conclusions. To address these gaps, we curated a large-scale, pathology-specific mul-timodal corpus comprising three public datasets and 660 au-thoritative pathology textbooks and educational notes. An overview of our data curation and model training pipeline is presented in Figure 2, with datasets for each training phase detailed below. For further information, refer to Appendix A. 

Continued Pretraining (CPT): Our CPT dataset com-prises a total of 3.5 million image-text pairs , of which 2.8 million are from PubMed (Sun et al. 2024b), Quilt (Ike-zogwo et al. 2023), PathGen (Sun et al. 2025), and 0.7 mil-lion from pathology textbooks and notes. This dataset also serves as the training data for our Patho-CLIP. 

Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT): Our SFT dataset com-prises 500k samples drawn from 5 pathological sub-fields: histopathology, gross examination, immunohisto-chemistry (IHC), cytology, fluorescence in situ hybridiza-tion (FISH); with 3 distinct levels of Chain-of-Thoughts per subfield; spanning 4 downstream task types: descrip-tive analysis, complex reasoning, multi-turn conversations, multiple-choice questions. This results in a total of 60 data combination types. 

Reinforcement Learning: Our RL dataset comprises 10k diagnostic-oriented MCQs, aligned with the same five pathological subfields defined in the SFT stage. 

Continued Pretraining and Patho-CLIP Training 

Data Preparation. We first scanned pathology textbooks into high-resolution images and used DocLayoutYolo (Zhao et al. 2024) to segment each page into body text, figure pan-els, and captions. Figure-caption pairs were matched based on spatial layout, and captions were extracted using OCR. For multi-panel figures, we applied edge detection and label recognition to split composite images and align sub-figures with corresponding caption segments. To leverage the rich contextual information in textbook narratives, we also ex-tracted body text via OCR and used the Qwen-max model to automatically identify in-line references between figures and surrounding text, reducing the need for manual annotation. Further details are provided in Appendix A.2. 

Patho-CLIP Training. We adopt OpenAI-CLIP-B and CLIP-L as backbone architectures and design a two-stage progressive training paradigm inspired by (Sun et al. 2025) to build a cross-modal model with fine-grained morpho-logical representation and clinical pathology understanding. In Stage I, the model is initialized via contrastive learning on the PathGen-1.6M dataset, emphasizing tissue-cell mor-phology and spatial organization to establish high-resolution morphological priors. In Stage II, we retain these representa-tions while integrating PathGen-1.6M with Quilt-1M, Path-Cap, and our textbook-derived dataset, forming a compos-ite corpus of 3.5 million image–text pairs. Empirical results show that this approach significantly improves the model’s ability to capture tissue heterogeneity and achieves state-28420 of-the-art performance in semantic understanding and im-age–text retrieval on multiple pathology benchmarks. See Appendix B.1 for further training details. 

Patho-R1 Continued Pretraining. Continued pretrain-ing has proven effective for infusing domain-specific knowl-edge into pretrained language models (Parmar et al. 2024), often yielding performance on par with or exceeding that of larger models (Chen et al. 2024). To ensure broad coverage of pathological knowledge, we combined all publicly avail-able pathology datasets with our curated context-rich im-age–caption pairs. Given the scale of the dataset (3.5 million samples), we selected Qwen2.5VL-3B and Qwen2.5VL-7B as base models to balance model capacity and data volume. 

Invoking Reasoning Ability via SFT 

While continued pretraining on domain-specific corpora imparts domain knowledge, it can compromise a model’s instruction-following ability (Cheng, Huang, and Wei 2024). Our goals in this phase are twofold: (1) to restore instruction-following capabilities , and (2) to elicit reason-ing behavior aligned with pathological diagnostics . Unlike general tasks, pathological diagnosis follows a highly struc-tured, systematic process. Thus, a cost-effective data gener-ation strategy that balances granularity and diagnostic speci-ficity is essential for this training phase. We began by categorizing textbook-extracted im-age–caption pairs into pathology subfields. A small subset was manually labeled to train an EfficientNet-based (Tan and Le 2019) classifier. Predictions on unlabeled data were manually reviewed and corrected, then added back into training to iteratively improve classifier accuracy. From 10 initial subfields, we selected 5 with the highest data volume and clinical relevance for SFT: histopathology, gross exam-ination, IHC, cytology, and FISH. However, these subfields still lacked sufficient data diversity. To address this, inspired by (Zhang et al. 2023), we applied three-way K-means clus-tering within each subfield using joint image–caption em-beddings. Interestingly, clusters consistently reflected differ-ent difficulty levels, which we used to define three tiers of Chain-of-Thought reasoning: easy, medium, and hard. We then paired these 15 CoTs with 4 downstream task types: MCQ, detailed description, complex reasoning, and multi-turn conversation, resulting in 60 total prompts for SFT data generation. See Appendix A.3 for further details. We selected DeepSeek-R1 as our SFT data generation model for two main reasons. First, current VLMs are not yet adapted to the pathology domain, and their performance in pathological imagery understanding is not satisfactory. In our experiments, supplying them with image–caption–CoT prompt triplets did not outperform strong text-only models using just captions. Second, DeepSeek-R1 excels in multi-step reasoning and long-context understanding—both es-sential for our complex CoT-style prompts. However, this choice introduced challenges, such as language mixing in medical terms and repetitive outputs. To address these, we applied a quality control stage involving rule-based filtering and selective manual verification. The final cleaned dataset contains 500k samples. See Appendix B.2 for implementa-tion details.

The selected samples were then paired with diagnostic in-formation to generate MCQs. See Appendix A.4 for details. Reward Function Design. We design reward functions to guide model learning from structured and accurate re-sponses. For the Format Reward , we require the model to format its response using <think >... </think > for interme-diate reasoning steps and <answer >... </answer > for the final answer. A reward score of 1 is assigned if both tag pairs appear exactly once and no content exists outside these tags; otherwise, the score is 0. For the Accuracy Reward ,the content within the <answer >... </answer > tags is ex-tracted using regular expressions, and only exact matches to a valid option from the predefined set A, B, C, D, E, F are accepted. A reward score of 1 is assigned if the extracted answer matches the ground-truth label and 0 otherwise. For the Length-Aware Penalty , we implement a soft overlong punishment similar to the original work of DAPO. For more details on reward function design, see Appendix B.2. ## Experiments 

Zero-shot cross-modal retrieval 

We compared Patho-CLIP against ten baseline CLIP vari-ants: OpenAI-CLIP-B, OpenAI-CLIP-L, PLIP, PathCLIP, CONCH, PathGen-CLIP, PathGen-CLIP-L, QuiltNet, Pub-medCLIP, and MUSK. We evaluated each model on two image–caption pairing datasets: Archive, compiled in-house from diverse pathology texts, and ARCH (Gamper and Ra-jpoot 2021); performance was measured using Recall@K. As shown in Table 1, on ARCH, Patho-CLIP-L achieves the highest scores across all four Recall@K settings, obtaining mean i2t and t2i recalls of 62.28% and 60.33%, respectively, substantially outperforming the leading baseline, CONCH (50.71% and 52.73%); moreover, Patho-CLIP-B also sur-passes CONCH. In the Archive dataset, Patho-CLIP-B at-tains mean i2t and t2i recalls of 13.34% and 13.72%, respec-tively, whereas Patho-CLIP-L achieves 21.31% and 22.13%, far exceeding PubmedCLIP’s 9.18% and 8.51%. These re-sults demonstrate that the latent-space alignment strategy of Patho-CLIP confers state-of-the-art accuracy and robustness in cross-modal retrieval. 

Zero-shot image classification 

To demonstrate Patho-CLIP’s zero-shot classification per-formance, we evaluated it on five pathology datasets: LC-Lung and LC-Colon (Borkowski et al. 2019), WSSSLUAD (Han et al. 2022), SICAPv2 (Silva-Rodr´ ıguez 2020), and BMT (Welch et al. 2024). For each dataset, we augmented class labels with synonyms (e.g., in LC-Colon dataset, the “colon adenocarcinoma” class was enriched with terms such as “adenocarcinoma of the colon” and “colorectal adenocar-cinoma”) and constructed category-specific text templates (e.g., “an H&E image of CLASSNAME.”, “an image of CLASSNAME.”). As shown in Table 2, Patho-CLIP-L surpasses CONCH by 30.75% on WSSSLUAD dataset and by 7.73% on SICAPv2 dataset, achieving the highest accuracy across all five datasets with a mean accuracy of 76.14%. No-tably, on BMT dataset, Patho-CLIP-B attains 49.33% ac-curacy—exceeding Patho-CLIP-L and substantially outper-forming QuiltNet (33.83%) and PathGen-CLIP (37.17%). The strong performance of Patho-CLIP underscores the di-versity of our pathology training data and validates our two-stage progressive training paradigm for enhancing semantic understanding in CLIP models.

Few-shot image classification with linear probing 

We also discuss the effectiveness of the model in few-shot learning, which is fine-tuned by linear probing of the pre-extracted feature representations of the model. We selected two representative datasets, LC-Lung and BMT, for testing under different training sizes of 2, 8, 16, 32, 64 and 128 samples, each of which implements 10 random samples. For each sample setting, we run 10 independent experiments, and finally the performance of the model under multivari-ate conditions is visualized by box-and-line plots. The experimental results show that the Patho-CLIP se-ries demonstrates excellent performance in extreme low-sample scenarios, providing a viable solution for annotation-poor clinical environments. Open-ended Benchmarks Our proposed model, Patho-R1, is designed to perform step-by-step reasoning and pro-duce accurate final answers. As shown in Figure 4, exist-ing general-purpose and medical models do not necessar-ily possess the reasoning capability required to correctly answer pathology-specific questions. To evaluate its per-formance, we follow a fuzzy evaluation strategy in which Deepseek-R1 acts as the LLM-judge, scoring generated out-puts based on ten well-defined criteria (Thawakar et al. 2025). Evaluations are conducted using the VLMEvalKit (Duan et al. 2024) framework on two publicly available pathology VQA datasets: Quilt-VQA and Path-VQA. This setup ensures consistency, reproducibility, and fair com-parison with other models. Our comprehensive evaluation highlights the strength of Patho-R1 in advancing multi-modal reasoning in pathology. In addition to the traditional CoT prompts, we also used the latest Chain-of-Draft (CoD) prompts (Xu et al. 2025) to explore whether simplifying the reasoning chain affects model performance. Our experimental results in Figure 5 show that Patho-R1 achieves the highest accuracy on both the Quilt VQA and Path VQA open-ended question-answering datasets, while also ranking highly in reasoning quality. An interesting find-ing is that CoD prompts do help improve answer accu-racy, especially on the relatively simpler Quilt VQA dataset, where the overall accuracy is higher. 

## Conclusion 

In this study, we proposed a data curation pipeline for the three phases of pathology VLM training. Leveraging this pipeline, we constructed a corpus comprising 3.5 million image–text pairs, 500k reasoning-enhanced SFT samples, and 10k diagnosis-oriented MCQs for reinforcement learn-ing. We further developed Patho-R1 and Patho-CLIP, two open-source models adapted via end-to-end domain trans-fer of pretrained VLMs. These models advance pathology-specific vision–language research and lay a foundation for future development across diverse clinical applications. 28424 Acknowledgments
