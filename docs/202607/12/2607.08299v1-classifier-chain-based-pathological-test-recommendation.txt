Abstract —Accurate and timely diagnoses are essential for quality patient care. However, delayed recommendation of di-agnostic tests and physicians’ subjective interpretations can hinder effective care. This study introduces a pathological test recommendation system that speeds up the test selection pro-cess using patient symptoms before physician consultation. The recommendation task is framed as a multi-label classification problem utilising the Classifier Chain (CC) technique to consider dependencies between tests. We collected data from the SOUTH-ERN.IML pathology and then created a custom dataset with the help of the expertise. Multiple machine learning algorithms, including Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, and Random Forest, were applied to compare models and identify the best fit for our study context. The Logistic Regression with CC model had the highest overall accuracy at 98.83%, while the Majority Voting ensemble model provided the best balance with a precision of 0.93, recall of 0.85, and F1-score of 0.89. To ensure transparency of the models and clinical interpretability, we used Explainable AI (XAI) techniques utilising SHAP (SHapley Additive Expla-nations), which identifies how each symptom is contributing to a test recommendation. The diagnostic reasoning revealed by the model was consistent with established medical knowledge of symptoms for the recommended tests, which further adds confidence to the model’s reliability for diagnostic purposes. The reasoning could help physicians make logical decisions in critical scenarios. Overall, our findings suggest that CC can improve the efficiency of the traditional algorithms in diagnostic process providing accurate test recommendations. 

Keywords —Pathological Test, Multi-label Classification, Classi-fier Chain (CC), Ensemble Learning, Explainable AI 

I. INTRODUCTION 

A better treatment for patients depends on an accurate and timely diagnosis. However, the current diagnostic process is often inefficient and requires patients to visit the doctor multiple times, resulting in delayed decision-making. This prolongs treatment cycles and strains the medical systems. Furthermore, the test recommendation is controlled by the physicians, making the process subjective and inconsistent. Sometimes, unnecessary tests are prescribed, while critical ones are missed. This refers to delays in treatment and increased costs. We propose a machine learning system to recommend appropriate pathological tests based on a patient’s symptoms. Automating this process could reduce diagnosis delays, leading to better patient care, create a real-life dataset of patient’s histories, and make doctors’ jobs easier. Our research targets a clear gap that most machine learning approaches in healthcare do not address. Most of them predict the disease directly without using any reliable pathological test and eliminating the involvement of any expert. This research aims to explore the intermediate step in predicting symptom-based tests for timely, accurate diagnosis, and improved care. Currently, the pathological test suggestion is solely dependent on the physician’s decision, which can be subjective to their expertise and experience. By developing a system that rec-ommends pathological tests, our aim is to support physicians, minimise inefficiencies, and improve the overall quality of care through advanced healthcare automation. Although machine learning has been widely used in disease prediction and medical image analysis, less effort has been put into the optimisation of ordering pathological tests. Several research studies have applied machine learning techniques such as decision trees and random forests in disease prediction; however, none of them have considered the complexities involved in multi-label classification for test recommenda-tion [1]. That also points to the limitations of single-label clas-sifications, pointing towards the necessity of advanced multi-label classifications. Our research builds on existing work by applying the Classifier Chain (CC) technique [2], which encodes dependencies between multiple test recommendations to provide improved predictions. Our study formulates test recommendations as a multi-label classification problem, where multiple tests can be ordered for a given set of symptoms. To address dependencies between tests, we apply the CC approach, which enchains classifiers sequentially to handle label dependencies. A feature dataset of 143 symptoms and 80 tests was created with the help of senior healthcare personnel. Different machine learning algorithms, such as Logistic Regression, Decision Trees and Random Forests [3], were compared based on performance to determine the required tests. An ensemble method using Majority Voting was also applied to tap the strength of all classifiers, hence making the system stronger. We used Explainable AI (XAI) techniques to ensure transparency and interpretability of the model as a prerequisite for its use in clinical scenarios. Based on SHAP analysis, our system illustrates how different symptoms contribute to the recommendations for diagnostic pathological tests. Explainability helps doctors understand the reasoning behind predictions, building trust and aligning with the medical principle of grounding in existing knowledge. By integrating predictive accuracy and interpretability, the system will not only automate decision-making for the diagnostic test recommendation process in primary care. However, it will also reveal test recommendations that are transparent, clinically relevant, and ethically defensible. Machine learning is now the foundation of current health-care research with a large number of diagnostic and predictive systems. Perhaps, it is most beneficial when computing is used for predicting disease from patients’ presenting symptoms, which has improved clinical decision-making using evidence-based data. Although we bypass additional advances in disease classification and prediction of patient conditions, the recom-mendation of pathological tests based on presenting symptoms remains unaddressed. The motivation of specifying systems that are able to predict possible tests of interest, particularly a recommended test, demonstrates the need for systems that offer modular testing, if not to show combinations of potential test results, and specifically using multi-label learning systems to model co-dependent relationships of multiple diagnostic outcomes. A system developed using the Decision Tree, Random For-est, and Na¨ ıve Bayes classifiers, using the Kaggle symptoms-to-disease dataset [4], which achieved approximately 95% accuracy. On the other hand, Decision Tree, Na¨ ıve Bayes, and SVM models used to predict heart disease, achieving similar results to the UCI StatLog dataset[5]. Another GUI-based Na¨ ıve Bayes and Decision Tree model developed to predicts diseases interactively[6], and a Django-based system combining Na¨ ıve Bayes, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, and KNN for disease and doctor recommendations proposed by [7]. Other works, such as [8] and [9], used neural networks and ensemble learning, respectively, to improve diagnostic ac-curacy through feature optimisation and dimension reduction. Despite these advances, all of these frameworks addressed single-disease or binary classification tasks and did not con-sider interdependencies between multiple diagnostic outcomes or tests. Subsequent research aimed to refine the predictive accu-racy, but still treated the results independently. An AI Therapy system developed in [10], which is a Decision tree–based symptom-disease predictor. In contrast, [11] combined SVM-RFE and Gain Ratio feature selection to improve the accuracy of Random Forest and Na¨ ıve Bayes to 84%. [12] expanded prediction to multiple diseases using six algorithms—including KNN, SVM, and Logistic Regression—yet their framework remained multi-class rather than multi-label, limiting its abil-ity to recommend several pathological tests simultaneously. Similarly, the authors [9], [13], [14], and [15] focused on opti-mising model performance for specific diseases; however, they mainly approached the task as independent disease prediction. Collectively, these works underscore the prevailing reliance on single-label classification paradigms in earlier healthcare research. In contrast, multi-label classification learning explicitly captures correlations among the multiple outputs, a capability pioneered by [2] through the use of the CC technique and later extended in ensemble (ECC) form. Further advancements, such as Multi-Label k-Nearest Neighbor (ML-KNN) [16], and Ensemble of Classifier Chains (ECC) [2], have demon-strated the robustness of multi-label classification strategies for handling label dependencies. Building on this foundation [17], they developed multi-label active learning for the pre-diction of heart-disease, achieving modest gains in the F1-score. In contrast, [18] used Random Forest with Dynamic Rough Set reduction for clinical decision support, still limited to single-label classification. Despite progress in algorithmic sophistication, none of these works addressed the problem of pathological test recommendation—an essential intermediate diagnostic task requiring the prediction of multiple interrelated pathological tests from a single symptom. This gap motivates our present study, which applies the CC framework with en-semble learning to automate pathological test recommendation, capturing label dependencies and enhancing the interpretability and realism of medical AI systems. 

Research Gap and Present Study 

In the studies summarised above, machine learning has demonstrated substantial progress in improving diagnostic accuracy through various classifiers and ensemble techniques. However, a clear research gap remains: most prior work framed disease diagnosis as a single-label classification problem, pre-dicting only one disease or outcome per instance. In con-trast, real clinical practice requires physicians to recommend multiple pathological tests simultaneously, many of which are interdependent. None of the reviewed studies explicitly modelled these label correlations, nor did they employ multi-label learning techniques such as CC to automate test recom-mendations based on co-occurring symptoms. To address this gap, our present study introduces a multi-label classification framework built on the CC approach, which enables the model to capture dependencies among pathological tests and also recommend multiple relevant pathological tests for a given set of symptoms reported by the patients. Using CC with an ensemble learning technique, our proposed system bridges the methodological divide between disease prediction and pathological test recommendation. This approach also improves clinical efficiency by minimising diagnostic delays, reducing unnecessary pathological tests, and supporting physi-cians through transparent, data-driven decision assistance. The objective of our study is to develop a machine learn-ing–based system that can recommend suitable pathological tests based on self-reported symptoms of a patient. In the usual diagnostic process, a patient often needs to visit a healthcare facility several times — first for an initial consultation, then for diagnostic tests such as blood work or imaging, and finally for a follow-up review. This repetitive process delays diagnosis, increases costs and effort for patients, and puts extra pressure on healthcare resources. A. Dataset Description 

We collected data from the SOUTHERN.IML pathol-ogy(Table I) and then created a custom dataset with the guidance of medical professionals to ensure symptoms and tests relevant to patient care. The dataset consists of 223 columns, including 143 symptom attributes (for example: itching, chills, joint pain) and 80 corresponding pathological tests (for example: complete blood count, vitamin B12, urine RME, liver function tests). Each row was expressed as a binary vector, where a 1 indicates that the patient has the symptom or the test is prescribed, and a 0 indicates the absence of the symptom and the test. To ensure generalisation for all types of patients, we shuffled the data and randomly partitioned it into multiple parts (training 70%, and test 30%), which ensures the balance of the diverse combination of symptoms in the dataset and reduces overfitting.

C. Classifier Chain(CC) Technique 

We employed a CC strategy to tackle the multi-label aspect of predicting pathology tests. Rather than employ en-tirely independent binary classifiers for each test label, CC builds a sequential chain of classifiers, each of which uses the original input features and the predicted outputs of all earlier classifiers as additional input. This structure helps the model capture contextual dependencies among related tests, thus improving its learning and overall predictive perfor-mance. The improvement supports the idea that explicitly modelling dependencies between pathological tests provides more contextually relevant predictions. For example, the liver function test and the bilirubin test are often ordered together in clinical practice, and the CC framework effectively captures this dependency—something independent classifiers are unable to achieve. However, since medical datasets are often class-imbalanced—with certain pathological tests occurring far less frequently than others—the CC framework is integrated with ensemble strategies to mitigate this issue. Specifically, the use of majority-voting ensembles ensures that minority test labels receive adequate representation during training, thereby improving both the robustness and the fairness of the model’s predictions. Let X = [ x1, x 2, . . . , x n] denote the input features (symp-toms) and Y = [ y1, y 2, . . . , y m] the target labels (pathological tests), where yj ∈ {0, 1}. Each training instance (xi, y i)

corresponds to a patient with a subset of required tests Yi ⊆ Y.The learning objective is to induce a function 

f : X → 2Y (1) that maps symptom profiles to the set of recommended tests. In the CC approach, we train a sequence of m binary classifiers (h1, h 2, . . . , h m) such that each hj predicts yj using both the original features and the predictions of the preceding classifiers: 

hj (x) = P (yj | x, y 1, y 2, . . . , y j−1) . (2) This type of sequential dependency enables the model to learn label correlations , which are crucial in medical scenarios where tests are often prescribed dependently. 3 | P a g e Under Review

1) Correlation-Based Ordering: Before model training, the test labels were pre-processed and arranged in order of their pairwise correlation coefficients. The higher correlated test labels were arranged closer to the beginning of the CC so that their influence would be included in all of the following predictors and increase the consistency and interpretability of the downstream predictions. 

2) Sequential Training: The classifiers in the chain are trained sequentially. The first classifier uses only the symp-toms’ features, while the subsequent classifiers use both the symptom inputs and the outputs for each of the previous classifiers. This incremental process of learning is useful in that the algorithm can recognise these cumulative relationships of related tests and simulate a physician’s decision-making process. 

D. Multi-Class Label Adaptation 

Although CC is natively binary per label, rare multi-class outputs were handled with a one-vs-rest decomposition, recoding each multi-class target into multiple binary labels. This allows the CC framework to process all targets uniformly while preserving the overall multi-label setting. 

E. Ensemble Learning (Majority Voting) 

To enhance robustness, we combined the outputs of CC-DT, CC-RF, and CC-LR through Ensemble Learning (Majority Voting) [20],[21] at the label level. For each pathological test, the final prediction corresponds to the class receiving at least two of the three votes from the base models. This integration mitigates individual model biases and also improves the sta-bility and generalisation across heterogeneous patient profiles. 

F. Model Selection and Base Learners 

To evaluate the robustness of the CC framework, we selected three common classifiers as base learners: 

• Decision Tree (DT) is selected for interpretability and its ability to model non-linear relations, providing transparent decision paths aligned with clinical reason-ing. 

• Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble of decision trees that reduces variance and overfitting, yielding a strong generalisation across diverse symptom profiles. 

• Logistic Regression (LR) is a probabilistic linear classifier that offers a stable, competitive baseline for binary decisions with efficient training. 

G. Training Procedure 

Each CC was trained on 70% of the dataset, with the remaining 30% reserved for testing. Before training, all records were randomly shuffled in each run to expose the models to various symptom–test patterns and reduce ordering bias. The feature set was standardised to zero mean and unit variance to ensure consistent model convergence. In the CC framework, we initially trained the first class on its original symptom features. Then we trained each following classifier on the same features as the previous classifiers, but included the binary predictions from all previous classifiers to help model label dependencies. Finally, we note that this sequential framework emulates the medical reasoning process in which the results of a given diagnostic test may influence the likelihood or need for another diagnostic test. We also empirically performed hyperparameter tuning for each base learner (Decision Tree, Random Forest, and Logistic 4 | P a g e Under Review 

Regression) through repeated trials to achieve balanced perfor-mance between precision and recall. To ensure reliability, each experiment was repeated with different random seeds and the final reported results represent the average performance across all runs. IV. RESULTS

The results illustrated the effect of the CC framework for capturing the label relationships compared to the conventional single-label modelling method.

B. Comparative Analysis and Discussion 

Cross-model performance suggests: 

• The Logistic Regression with CC had the best rates for the quantitative metrics, making it the most viable model for a base classification in the multi-label prediction scenario. 

• The Random Forest with CC strongly dilated robust-ness and interpretability, and it performed consistently in classification decisions in the imbalanced datasets. 

• The Decision Tree without CC performed the worst, demonstrating the limitations of employing single independent classifiers in multi-label diagnostics in medical scenarios. 

• The Majority Voting Ensemble achieved a very fa-vorable compromise between precision and recall and produced stable performance even for distributional skew. In summary, the use of a CC structure has a positive impact on predictive accuracy when coupled with an en-semble learning method. Our system appropriately recognises the appropriate pathological tests for the clinically associated conditions based on the patient’s symptoms. The implications of these studies suggest that the framework has the potential to serve as a solid foundation for clinical assistance in an early stage to improve medical reasoning. V. EXPLAINABLE AI R ESULTS 

To improve the transparency and interpretability of our proposed model for recommending pathological tests, we applied a unified analysis of SHAP ( [22]) to evaluate the contribution of individual symptoms to each model prediction. Nominally, relative to the classic estimate of global weighting or influence established on feature importance( [23]), SHAP adds a deeper explanation of the predictions through a coop-erative game-theoretic methodology and a more unified and principled approach. Except as trait attribution in a prediction, SHAP provides the output of the model (the contribution to the pathological test recommendation) as additive feature attributions, making possible concepts that bring in symptoms specifically for pathological test prediction, both globally and specifically. This Fig. 4 depicts the SHAP feature-importance values for the ANA (Antinuclear Antibody) test prediction model. The analysis indicates that Nodal Skin Eruptions and Joint Pain were the two symptoms that had the highest influence, contributing equally but in a positive direction. This aligns with clinical intuition, as dermatological and musculoskeletal manifestations are often primary indicators of autoimmune activity, which typically warrant an ANA test. Other symp-toms, such as stomach pain, bone pain, and skin rashes, had a moderate influence; however, this remained consistent with their secondary contribution to the autoimmune diagnostic pathway. 5 | P a g e Under Review

In general, the interpretability analysis based on SHAP provides an transparent explanation of the internal decision-making of the model. By identifying clinically meaningful predictors and understanding their relative contribution, we increase confidence in the diagnostic capacity of the model and its potential future use within real-world medical decision support applications. Furthermore, this explainability demonstrates that the model not only achieves high, or appropriate, levels of real-world accuracy but also conforms to acceptable levels of interpretability for clinical practice, which are important attributes for accepting models into the predictive space and for regulatory approval. VI. DISCUSSION 

Our research has clearly shown that it is possible for the model to recommend pathological tests, as well as handle the multi-label classification task using the CC approach to enhance predictive accuracy and clinical relevance. The CC model also took into account the dependencies between tests and generated contextually suitable predictions similar to real diagnostic reasoning. Compared to the model’s result, Logistic Regression with CC outperformed the others and achieved the highest test accuracy, indicating a strong level of robustness and accuracy in generalising to new cases, and even more importantly, its ability to efficiently deal with correlated features. Random Forest with CC performed comparably, achieved balanced precision and recall values, and the Majority Voting ensemble significantly improved upon the two individual classifiers, proving to be more robust. The results show that the modelling of label dependencies and ensemble learning as a support for modelling label dependencies were robust and reliable beyond patients with their different symptoms. The most important thing is that our proposed methodology maintains largely the same performance for evaluating real-world data on imbalanced datasets, where common tests are induced over rare tests. This methodology’s ability to predict accurately under natural data imbalance without the use of synthetic resampling provided further evidence that it successfully learned from true diagnostic relationships while maintaining clinical realism. The implications of robustness from a clinical relevance and predictive accuracy value demonstrate a strong potential to consider its use in healthcare workflows where frequency rates of tests vary significantly between patient populations. SHAP analysis from XAI offered an extra layer of validity to the model’s clinical validity. The findings clearly support interpretability, providing further transparency and clinically useful information to enhance clinician trust in AI-supported clinical decision making. Although the findings are optimistic, limitations still exist. Because we developed a dataset with the help of many physicians from different medical hospitals, external validity may limit the generalisability of the findings across geograph-ical regions. Future investigations should strive for multi-site 6 | P a g e Under Review 

datasets and consider advanced dependency models, such as graph-based or deep multi-label architectures, if finer rela-tionships might be identified. Moreover, finally, expand the explainability, using other XAI techniques such as SHAP to provide additional transparency. In summary, our proposed frameworks for CC, ensemble learning, and explainable AI can provide reliable support for intelligent, transparent, and clinically usable diagnostic decisions. The high accuracy of the model under true real-world conditions of imbalance reveals its time to deploy within healthcare systems today. VII. CONCLUSION 

Our research presents a pathological test recommendation framework using CC and ensemble learning to enhance pre-diction accuracy by better mapping diagnostic test dependen-cies. The suggested multi-label approach goes beyond the traditional single-label disease prediction by modelling the natural cooccurrence of medical tests during clinical decision-making processes. The results confirmed that the CC-based approach generates a better prediction than independent binary classifiers in each evaluation scenario. It has clinical relevance for healthcare applications, as it reduces diagnostic delays, minimises unnecessary laboratory tests, and improves the overall workflow. Explainable AI (SHAP) analysis confirmed that the model’s decision logic closely aligns with clinical pathology reasoning, enhancing understandability and trust. Next steps should focus on validating the model using large, multi-institutional datasets, evaluating a deep multi-label architecture to capture higher-order relationships, and prospectively assessing performance in real clinical scenarios. With continuous development, this pathological test recom-mendation framework has the potential to become a deployable AI-based clinical decision support tool, providing value to patients and physicians through more timely and accurate data-driven medical assessments.
